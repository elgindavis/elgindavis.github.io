Foxy birds

A closer look at the process of creating my first iOS game

idea/concept

Foxy Birds is an endless-runner style iOS game that I created and released in June of 2018. I developed and designed the entire game from scratch to gain experience in taking a game from idea to market. My main focus was to quickly create an enjoyable gaming experience catering to a certain demographic, and also to learn more about the process of design, development, and production - especially as they relate to video games.

research

For my initial research, I looked to quick and catchy games, such as the likes of Flappy Birds (which also had a definite influence in coming up with the name Foxy Birds). Seeing as the audience for these types of games is fairly broad, I began my approach to developing this game with a similarly broad audience. 

design and test

At the outset of the project I searched for a while for simple games styles that would be quick and simple to make, and decided on the classic endless-runner style as a starting point. After nailing down my game experience idea, I began to wireframe on paper and flesh-out all of the details that I wanted to add. 

User Flow

After several iterations of user flows through the app, I decided to cut most of the extra features at the outset and landed on a simple, 2-page flow for the game.

Prototyping Gameplay

At this stage, I imagined various methods of gameplay to make a memorable and exciting in-game experience, landing on one that forces the player to use moving obstacles to their advantage.

Combining Retro and Modern

For the game's look and feel, I combined a modern, simple interface with some retro elements, like the black-and-blue start button that resembles the arcade games of the 80s to create a unique pairing in the interface.

Testing

I conducted usability testing for the game with a number of users by watching them play and having them narrate the experience aloud as I took notes.

build and deploy

I built the game over the course of a few weeks in the Godot game engine, leaving the nice-to-have features for further development and sticking only to the minimum viable product. An artist's work is never done, so be on the lookout for a Foxy Birds remake coming in 2019!

My Daily bread

A Christian-based, note-taking mobile experience

idea/concept

My Daily Bread is a mobile app developed in Ionic to allow Christians to take notes on certain scriptures as they engage with the Word of God every day.

research

The initial research for this project came informally over the course of a few years. The process starts with an observation of a potential problem. When I would go to church on Sundays, I would notice people taking their notes on the sermon and scriptures in paper journals and notepads. When the service ended, they would tuck away their notepads, and in most cases, probably not look at the notes again until the next Sunday. While some people take notes simply because it helps them retain information better whether they ever look at the notes again or not, I saw that there might be more to it.

design and test

my daily bread v2

A basic, low-fidelity prototype of the in-app experience during the re-design process. 

User Flow

I started the design of the app by thinking through all of the different possibilities of actions that a user might take, and what their respective interfaces might look like. The key step here was to differentiate the minimum viable product features from the nice to have features.

Wireframing

After narrowing down my scope, I went through a few rounds of wireframing the screens to show the user flow and possible actions that the users may take in navigating the app. I continued to refine this flow until I landed on a flow that appears minimal enough to let the user take full control of their experience.

Prototyping the Experience

In the onboarding slides, there are options to login and/or sign up, which the user will be prompted for later when they try to save notes. However, the key point for this flow is that creating an account is not required for a user who simply wants to read scriptures in the app. 

User Journey

As a part of my research, I created a user journey based on "Think, Do, Feel" progressions of a user. This allowed me to draw some concrete thoughts from a potential user, and better understand their needs and goals that might lead them to download this app.

Jobs-to-be-done

In this stage, I noted the jobs that the user needs to get done in the app, and took the process a step further by questioning why each job was important, and then answering why it is important to better understand the user's needs.

Testing

Usability testing with the prototype and in-app data collection with Google Analytics were my main methods of testing the user experience of the app. Both helped me gain honest insight into the app's experience, as well as solid steps to improve the experience overall.

build and deploy

I considered the possibilities of storing everything locally versus storing information in the cloud, and I concluded that local storage for the actual entry information was too volatile, seeing as a user can clear the whole phone and lose all of their data, or get a new phone and not have access to any of their saves reflections. So I then decided to research some different cloud storage options, and landed on Google Firebase as my best option for its flexibility and size of the database. Every user would sign up for the app, saving email and password, and all of their entries would be saved to their personal, randomized userID key in the database.
I decided on Ionic as the framework of choice for this project because making an Ionic app requires a basic knowledge of HTML, CSS, and Javascript, and I already had experience with all three. In addition, Ionic allows for deployment of a mobile app to both iOS and Android from a single code base, though there are obviously performance and customization tradeoffs between native and cross-platform development.
I decided to use an open source version of ESV and store it inside the app so that the app would not require internet connectivity to read scripture.

weather bible

Taking an app idea from start to finish in just 3 days

Weather Bible is a small app project I made over the course of a few days in January of 2018.

Idea/Concept:
The idea behind Weather Bible was to create a simple interface that would allow the user to see the current weather for any city along with a bible verse that relates to that form of weather.

Major design decisions:
Restraining app to 3 screens - there could have been many screens and features added to this app, but my solution was easily implemented in just 3 screens, saving resources, time, and energy during the initial design and development process.
Having users input their own location- by opting to have the user input their own location, the app is able to allow the user full control of their data, so their phone's location does not need to be on and tracking during use of the app.
Color scheme- the color scheme of the app was inspired by the idea of a night time city using cool, calming colors, especially blue which is commonly associated with water, weather, rain, etc.

Major Takeaways:
An app idea doesn't always have to be complicated, or even intended to make money. Sometimes we have ideas that are just fun to realize, and we can learn a lot in the process of doing so.
The best design sometimes goes unnoticed!
API keys with limited quotas are an interesting case, so sometimes us developers have to get a bit creative!
I learned a great deal about color pyschology over the course of this project, and how each color choice goes a long way in creating a certain look, feel, and vibe of an experience.
Ionic is a very low-cost solution to make apps quickly, gain experience, and gain quick feedback.
