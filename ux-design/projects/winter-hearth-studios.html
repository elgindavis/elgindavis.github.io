Winter Hearth Studios Progressive Web App

Design, Development, Content-production

I gave my studio's content a cozy place to live on the internet

Desiging a Content Platform

Context

Winter Hearth Studios is my own creative studio, where I aim to teach, entertain, and inspire others with a focus on mental and emotional health. I run the studio solo, and do all of the writing, branding, graphics and illustrations, music (for video games and podcasts), social media, and programming (website and video games) myself.

I designed and coded a simple platform for Winter Hearth Studios to be able to gather all of the studio’s content in one place, rather than scattered across the web on Apple Podcasts and Spotify, Medium, Facebook, Instagram, Mailchimp, and YouTube.

I was in a bit of a time crunch to get the site up and running quickly, because I wanted to capitalize on the momentum I had gained from my weekly newsletters. For this reason, I didn’t spend as much time producing high-fidelity mockups as I otherwise would have. I also wanted to provide an app-like experience for power users who wanted it and could benefit from it, so I created the website as a progressive web app.

Process

The first thing I mapped out was a way to display all of my existing content. I ran an email newsletter series in 2019 on mental health, but all of the content lived on Mailchimp’s servers and in their archive page. I also wanted to be able to have an easy way to incorporate my own self-hosted blog, with the ability to easily find and listen to the studio’s podcast, future videos, and future art galleries that I envisioned at the outset.

for mobile and desktop

After some basic wireframe sketches and a bare-bones Figma mockup, I started designing the site’s layout using ReactJS and GatsbyJS, with a mobile-first process and dark-mode as the default preference.

I originally designed the site to be able to change between light and dark mode, but after some user testing with friends and family, I found that leaving the site in the dark setting without a switch was a better solution.

The blog posts and newsletters are content that I wanted to be searchable as well, so I made a really simple search component so the user is able to find what they are looking for, whether on the home page, blog page, or newsletters page.

I also wanted the ability to highlight specific content when a user is already reading something, so I added a popular post column, as is common for content platforms to do to solve this problem.

Challenges

The biggest challenge was to find a solution that works specifically for my goals. I wanted to be able to create videos, podcast episodes, illustrations, articles, newsletters, and anything else, and aggregate everything from the studio in one place without the website looking chaotic and being confusing to navigate. I did rigorous sitemap planning before putting the site together, in order to solve that problem.

Another big challenge I faced was time. In transitioning from writing newsletters to writing articles, a weekly email format was no longer conducive to my objectives. I wanted to have the site up and running in time to capitalize on the momentum I had gained in my newsletters before the newsletters faded from the memory of my audience.

Outcome

I have received a lot of good feedback on the site and its usability, and especially the navigability of the content. Many folks also dig the late-night arcade-like color scheme, which I also really like as a design decision. I’ve made continued improvements since then, especially to the grid displays and the in-article appearance. I was really satisfied how the site turned out, and it was finished in time to capitalize on that transition from weekly emails to articles and now the podcast. Future work will include adding space for my comedy skits and an illustration gallery.
