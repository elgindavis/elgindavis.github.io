Process

From start to finish, this is how we chose to analyze our data and draw meaningful conclusions.

Roadmap

Problem: What are the major factors to consider when analyzing the crime rates of metropolitan statistical areas in various regions of the United States?
This is the path that we followed to realize our data undertaking.

Step 1: Gather Data

FBI & Census Data

To begin our project, we downloaded the excel files with data on murder, provided for each year from the FBI's Uniform Crime Reporting Program. We found that there were several metrics for data, but given that each MSA's population is different, we decided that murder rate per 100,000 inhabitants would be the best metric to use as our dependent variable. We then moved to the Census Factfinder page to gather the data that we would later use as our predictors.

Step 2: Parse and Clean Data

Pandas and Dataframes

In this step, we create a function utilising the pandas Dataframe type to parse the FBI murder rate data sets for the years 2006-2016 and to create a clean, readable data collection that we could then work with. After this, we created a similar function that would parse each data set of predictors (marital status, unemployment rate, etc.) for each year and create a usable data collection like that of the FBI sets. We were then able to merge the data sets by year to account for each predictor and each metropolitan statistical area.

Step 3: Initial Exploratory Data Analysis

Change Over Time

After creating these huge data sets for each year,we decided to create some visual representations to see exactly what we had managed to find so far. You can find some of these here. 

Step 4: Congregating Data                         

The Merger

After learning that EDA over time would likely require knowledge of 'Time Series' and its implications, we opted to coalesce our dataframes from each year into a single dataframe that would be representative of the entire country's MSA's and their murder rates and population attributes over the last decade.
As seen in the marriage data plots, many of the points in 2016 are misssing and we were unable to find any replacements. For our project, we decided to remove 2016 data and move forward examing the years 2006 to 2015. The all_data dataframe collapses the time element of our data and places everything into a single year. This way we are not looking at changes over time for specific predictors, but rather treating every MSA in every year as a single observation.

Step 5: Dividing the Data and Applying One-Hot Encoding

Divide and Conquer

In order to incorporate an element of geography into the data, we chose to divide up the data into 5 different regions, the Northeast, Southeast, Southwest, Midwest, and West. The state groupings can be seen below and it was our deicision to play certains states into the given region, though the may be multiple ways to categorize a specific state.
We also decided to one hot encode each MSA within the region. This will allow for some element of time to be incorporated into the modles because each MSA should have 10 points within the overall dataframe, provided that there was enough prediction data to collect for each of those years.

Step 6: Further EDA

Regional Observations

Having determined that examining each region separately would lead to more interesting findings, we decided to conduct further EDA, this time inspecting the visual relationships between each predictor and region separately.

Step 7: Dividing Training and Test Data

StellarTest Scores

A useful model, in our opinion, is one that can be used to predict future occurences. Therefore, we found that it would make sense to split our data such that the data from years prior to 2014 would be used to train the model, and we will test this model using the data from years 2014 and 2015.
We recognize that since we have not learned time series models, we are unable to build a model that can accurately take into account the trends for each region by year. However, this method of splitting train and test would give us insight into whether or not previous years as a whole can be informative of the forthcoming years' murder rates.

Step 8: Modeling Each Region

America's Next Top Model

Now that we have a slightly better understanding of what we see in our data based on our EDA, we now move on to modeling the data from each region with several different models. For our project, we used:
- Stepwise forward selection (baseline)
- Multiple Linear Regression
- Polynomial Regression
- Lasso & Ridge Regression
-  PCA

Step 9: Checking for Collinearity

The Heat(map)

Given our surprisingly high scores from the PCA, we suspected that perhaps our predictors may be collinear in a way that gives us falsely high PCA scores. Thus, we examined a heat map of our non-encoded predictors. We found that unsurprisingly, predictors such as median_age_f, median_age_m and median_age were all correlated. Additionally, although percent employed and percent unemployed are not necessarily the converse of each other, they were significantly negatively correlated.
However, given that we have a large number of predictors, we don't suspect that the fact that these groups were correlated would have had a significant impact on how high the test scores were. With that said, we do think that these correlated values may have had some impact on the scores that we observed.

Step: 10 Results & Conclusions

The Verdict

Overall, we found that for all regions of the US, using PCA analysis with a high number of components will result in a very high R2 value. We also found that out of all of the regions, the Southeast consistently has the highest murder rates across all of the predictors, and the West generally has the lowest. Our most significant predictors of murder rate across the 5 regions, from both visual and statistical analysis, were race, employment status, marital status, college degree attainment, income, and median age of females.
