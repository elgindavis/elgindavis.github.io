Crime in the City

In our data analysis, we compiled data from the past 11 years of FBI crime statistics and U.S. Census data for the major metropolitan statistical areas in the country. Our project seeks to answer the question,

"What are the major factors to consider when analyzing the crime rates of metropolitan statistical areas in various regions of the United States?"

Initial EDA: by Year

Before narrowing down the scope of our project, we wanted to examine the data by year. Although we do not have experience with time series, we thought it would be useful to examine trends and changes in trends of our chosen predictors year over year.

Below are some scatter plots visualizing the relationships between our major predictors and the murder rate of each metropolitan city separated by year.

College Education

The chart below shows the relationship between the amount of residents who have college degrees in a specific city and murder rate. Though we don't see any clear-cut lines of influence here, we do note that the cities with the highest percentages of college graduates correlate with generally lower murder rates across the country. As a whole, we also note an increase in cities with murder rates above 10% starting in 2012 and continuing through 2016. 

Love & Marriage

Despite studies revealing the increasing divorce rate in America, the data we have here suggests that the marriage percentage correlates negatively with the murder rates in the country. That is to say, we observe that cities with higher percentages of married couples have lower murder rates. This does not imply causality, but this does suggest some degree of significance for marriage as a predictor for violent  crimes in the U.S.

Out of Work

Another notable predictor we observed was the rate of unemployment throughout the country. Higher unemployment does seem to correlate with higher murder rates, but as we have seen so far, there are many factors that weigh in to the equation when it comes to analyzing such a data set.

Literature Review

A comprehensive study of key factors affecting the rate of violent crimes in metropolitan cities in the U.S.

The area of criminology and crime predictions is a popular topic that can be examined through a not only a data-science method, but also a sociological, politically scientific, or psychological lense. In the media, there is frequent coverage of artificial intelligence or machine learning related topics and how these new technologies will impact the criminal justice system to help law enforcement and communities better root out the source of crime, and eventually put it to an end. FiveThirtyEight published an article in 2015 entitled “Should Prison Sentences Be Based On Crimes That Haven’t Been Committed Yet?”, and while mainstream media articles similar to this attempt to shed light on current research in the predictive capabilities of crime data, the impact of the research as it relates to public policy is somewhat overblown.

However, FiveThirtyEight distills the model for easy interpretability, as it is meant for the general public, and its predictors of gender, age, race, and education have a high correlation to an individual’s likelihood of committing or recommitting a crime (Barry-Jester). The work University of Pennsylvania criminology researchers was summarized in Wired.com concerning how to predict a person’s future crimes based upon those commited in the past. The research cited showed that age and the type of crime commited were the best predictors of an individual’s likelihood to commit a crime in the future (Zetter). While the question we have chosen to answer in our project is on a macro-level versus individual prediction, the predictors used by much of the research is useful and shows that the data we have chosen should put us on a good path toward predicting murder rates in geographical areas.

In addition to mainstream media sources, there is a significant amount of academic texts that looks to predict or model crimes in cities and other geographical areas. In addition to common predictors such as race, age, and education, research concerning the geolocation of a person’s cell phone was used to classify certain areas as high-crime or low-crime (Bogomolov). The same researchers were able to find that using that mobile data in addition to the demographic data of the cell phone users, they were able to classify high-crime “hotspots” and communities with up to 70% accuracy (Bogomolov). Again, classification is not the type of problem that we are looking to answer, but an element of geography should be helpful in our methodology.

Other researchers at Iowa State university studied how to predict the type of murder committed relative to the relationships of the murderer and the victim. When building their models using various classification methods such as random forests and SVM, predictors such as state, age, month, and sex, were used to predict whether the murderer was a family member, close to the family, acquaintance, or stranger (Yang). The data used by the Iowa State researchers was from the FBI and UCR, which could make their work more impactful in our project.

The most interesting study that had serious repercussions in our data selections was David Lester’s work which studies methods of prediction for murder rates of police on civilians and vice versa. Lester’s work found that divorce rate and unemployment, two predictors that are less common in mainstream news articles and other technical analyses of crime and homicide, are two of the best for predicting the homicide rate in the United States (Lester). The time-series regression used by Lester et. al. is beyond what we have studied in class, but the effectiveness of these predictors gave us additional ideas about data to explore.
