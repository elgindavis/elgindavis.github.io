After training our data on 2006-2013, we use 2014 & 2015 to test the accuracy of each model by region. Below are some of the specifics for each model.



Stepwise Forward Selection (baseline)

Given that our one-hot encoding provided several predictors, we wanted to get an idea of which non-categorical predictors might be significant to predicting murder rate. Therefore, we used forward selection with only the non-categorical predictors as candidates

Stepwise Results




Multiple Linear Regression

Next we conducted multiple linear analysis using all the predictors, including the one hot encoded ones.

Multiple Linear Reg. Results




We found that multiple linear regression gave varied results for each region. For the Northeast and Midwest, R^2 went slightly down. However, for other regions, we were able to see some improvements.

Polynomial Regression

Given that we have quite a few numerical columns, we decided to try polynomial regression using these columns as well. We created polynomials to the power of 2 and included them into our regression analysis.

Polynomial Reg. Results



We found that our test R^2 scores generally went up. We recognize that simply having more predictors can sometimes falsely increase R^2 scores, however, this may also imply that the inclusion of polynomial predictors were able to create a more fitting model.

Ridge & Lasso Regressions

Our datasets do not have an overly high number of observations in the training set. However, even so, we recognize that overfitting is a concern we must keep in mind when looking at data sets. Therefore we tried fitting a Ridge and Lasso regression as well.

PCA

Given that our data has high dimensionality as a result of the one-hot encoding, we decided that reducing dimensions via PCA would be appropriate.

PCA Results





Collinearity Heatmap

Given our surprisingly high scores from the limitless PCA, we suspected that perhaps our predictors may be collinear in a way that gives us falsely high PCA scores. Thus, we examined a heat map of our non-encoded predictors. We found that unsurprisingly, predictors such as median_age_f, median_age_m and median_age were all correlated. Additionally, although percent employed and percent unemployed are not necessarily the converse of each other, they were significantly negatively correlated.

However, given that we have a large number of predictors, we don't suspect that the fact that these groups were correlated would have had a significant impact on how high the test scores were. That said, we do think that these correlated values may have had some impact on the scores that we observed.

Overall, we found that for all regions of the US, using PCA analysis with a high number of components will result in a very high R^2 value.
