My journey back to the Mamelodi township of South Africa

Mission in Mamelodi

A few years ago I felt called to go to Mamelodi, Pretoria (in the Gauteng providence of South Africa) to teach computer literacy to underprivileged students, but I had no idea how much I myself would learn and grow through my interactions with them and with the country at large. Seeing the faces of the students light up as they competed in typing contests or, for some, as they used a computer for the first time, completely changed my perspective on life. The fact that I had the ability to impact their lives in some way opened my eyes not only to the situation that was presented before me, but also to the much larger global issue at hand - a lack of opportunity.

Some of these students were the brightest I'd met of that age (11 years old), yet they didn't and still don't have the resources or education to really make their dreams into reality like many of us do in other countries. It was here that I found my passion for education and youth development, and where I decided that more on my part had to be done. We worked together for 2 weeks in January of 2017 on team-building, computer literacy, friendship, work ethic, learning and studying habits, and many other intangible skills.

What is the Mamelodi Initiative?

Mamelodi is a township outside Pretoria, South Africa that has been devastated by the AIDS pandemic and years of racial injustice and exploitation. In the Mamelodi Initiative, we partner with students and administrators at the University of Pretoria and local churches in the area to establish “at risk” youth in Mamelodi in a vital relationship with God and a solid Christ-centered church body.

The Mamelodi Initiative was created because students are very underserved in terms of available education in the township of Mamelodi. In the Gauteng providence, students must pass a test called the Matric in order to graduate from the equivalent of American high school, and to be able to apply to college. According to the Mamelodi Initiative,

Less than 50% of adults in Gauteng passed Matric, and research shows non-white South Africans are the most likely to drop out, making this issue even more urgent in township communities. Increased drop-out rates lead to higher unemployment, higher crime rates, and more reliance on the government.

My Resolve

When I return to South Africa, I will be working directly with these students again, pouring the great blessings and opportunities that I have been given back into the global community, serving these students and helping to strengthen their chances at better futures. I will have the incredible opportunity to share my knowledge, faith, passion, and vision with these students again to help them in their goal to learn, grow, dream, and achieve, and I hope you will support us in this mission!
